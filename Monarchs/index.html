British Monarchs

Who are the British Monarchs?

The British Monarchs have ruled over varying territories over the years thanks to the British Empire, however the sights were not always set on global domination. The topic of which countries should be under the rule of the King/Queen of England has always been slightly contentious.

The United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland - as it stands today - includes the countries of England, Northern Ireland, Scotland, and Wales.

The Anglo-Irish Treaty was made effective in 1922, granting sovereignty to the territory now known as the Republic of Ireland.

The Acts of Union 1800 were a series of parliamentary acts that merged the Kingdoms of Great Britain and Ireland.

The Acts of Union 1707 were a series of parliamentary acts that merged the Kingdoms of England and Scotland.

In 1649, Oliver Cromwell ordered the execution of King Charles I, effectively abolishing the monarchy. From 1653 to 1659, Oliver followed by his son Richard led the Commonwealth of England, Scotland and Ireland as Lord Protector. The monarchy was restored through King Charles II.

By 886, Alfred the Great possessed Wessex, Mercia, Kent, and the South East of England. After the defeat of the Danish King Guthrum as King of East Anglia, Alfred the Great styled himself as King of the English (as opposed to just King of Wessex like his predecessors).

* The English Bill of Rights 1689 dictated that Mary II and her husband William of Orange would co-rule the nation until one's death, whereupon the remaining diarch would assume complete rule. Mary died first, and William was left as sole ruler until his death.

† Cwichelm of Wessex is a monarch shrouded in some mystery. Sometimes he is labelled as a distinct King of Wessex, and other times he is conflated with the contemporarenous Cynegils as either the same person, or as the latter's son.
