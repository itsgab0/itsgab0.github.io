The Flood Myth

What is the Flood Myth?

Across almost every culture, we seem to be able to find one piece of heritage/myth wherein a large group of people faced some kind of divine intervention in the form of a great flood. As spoken tales go, there is a remarkable amount of consistency amongst all versions of this myth. Of course, to state they all came from the same myth would be a ludicrous leap, however it should be plain to see that one or several major floods influenced a lot people in ancient history, and the go-to explanation would be that it was deic in nature.

As stated prior, there is a reasonable amount of variability amongst the myths, such as the reason for the flood - some myths say it was an angry deity, others posit a great battle. Given the state of science as far back as the 3rd millenium BCE, it's not difficult to see why many suggested divine vengeance. If one understood that tides of oceans ebbed and flowed through the day, and during different parts of the year it tended to get higher than ever, and other times it was lower than ever - why then would one day it go so high that it starts taking lives?

However, cum hoc ergo propter hoc. Hand-in-hand with this belief comes the idea of worldbuilding. Stories and old wives tales are handed down via word of mouth, from generation to generation. All it takes is some personification here and euhemerisation there and suddenly it's not just a series of natural events - you have a proper tale with gods and goddeses, heroes and heroines. Take and use what you know, if you don't understand it - put it into terms you do understand.

The author can't and shan't attempt to justify nor explain the origins of any of the following myths. The above was merely a thought experiment to suggest possibilities, but should be regarded as pure fiction. Additionally, the author makes no attempt to approve nor disprove any body of myth. The border between myth and religion in terms of active worship is such that there are some myths in currently-practiced religions.

This is not an exhaustive list, and will be added to as and when the author has time.


Ziusudra Sumerian 29th-century BCE
The gods decided to send a flood to destroy mankind. Enki, the god of underworld seas warned Ziusudra (ruler of Shuruppak) to build a large boat. A terrible storm raged for 7 days and nights. Then Utu (Sun) appeared and Ziusudra opened a window, prostrated himself, and sacrificed an ox and a sheep to An (Sky) and Enlil (Lordbreath) who gave him ‘breath eternal’.


Atra-Hasis Babylonian 18th-century BCE
Enki (the god of water) warned the hero Atra-Hasis (‘extremely wise’) through the reeds of the chief god Enlil’s plan to destroy humanity by flood. He was told to dismantle his house and build a boat. He boarded the boat with his family and animals and the storm that scared even the gods began. After 7 days, the storm eased and Atra-Hasis made sacrifices to the gods. This alerted Enlil, who became angry at Enki – the latter stated they did not violate their oath.


Gun Yu Ancient Chinese 11th-century BCE
The great flood began during the reign of Emperor Yao, both the Yellow River and the Yangtze valleys were flooded. Yao appointed distant relative Gun (prince of Chong). He stole Xirang (self-expanding soil) from the Supreme Divinity – angering it. The magical soil didn’t seem to solve the problem, and the flood continued for 9 years. The emperor stood down for a proven man Shun, whose rule Gun questioned. After many years, Gun was banished for his failure and met his death. His son Yu, focused more on drainage rather than containment. After succeeding, he ascended to emperor, and founded the Xia dynasty.


Utnapishtim Mesopotamian 7th-century BCE
On the banks of the Euphrates, the god Ea (Enki) warned Utnapishtim of the great gods’ plans to flood the earth. Utnapishtim was ordered to build a boat to keep living beings alive. He boarded the boat with his family and animals, and sealed the boat shut. After 7 days, the storm eased and he let his livestock out. He sacrificed a sheep, alerting the gods. Enlil became angry at Ea, who deflected onto the hero. Enlil boarded the boat and granted Utnapishtim and his wife immortality.


Yima Zoroastrian 8th-century - 5th-century BCE
Ahura Mazda meets with Yima, a shepherd-turned-King chosen by Ahura Mazda to rule over the Earth, to warn of a harsh winter storm and then a great melt. Ahura Mazda instructs Yima to build a large Vara (enclosure). Yima kneads the earth into shape, making a small town which he populated with 2000 of the fittest of men and women, as well as two of every animal and plant. He created a light in the cavern and sealed it with a golden ring.


Noah Judaism, Christianity, Islam 8th-century - 1st-century BCE
Judaism, Christianity
Noah was a religious man who followed Yahweh. The latter saw the earth was corrupt and filled with violence, so bade Noah to build an ark for his family and a couple of each animal. At the age of 600, he entered his ark and Yahweh called down a great storm for forty days and nights until the highest mountains were covered to a depth of 15 cubits. Each drop of rain was passed through Gehenna such that they became hot and scalded the non-believers, who had been made hot by their sensual desires. After 150 days, Yahweh ceased the rain and made a covenant with Noah that man may eat every living thing but not its blood and promised not to destroy all life by a flood.
Islam
Noah, an Islamic prophet, received a message from Allah that he had spread his message as far as he could, and no-one else would believe him. Noah prayed that Allah would rid the earth of sinners, and Allah decreed a terrible flood, ordering Noah to construct a ship that would save the believers.


Deucalion Ancient Greek 3rd-century BCE
Zeus was angered by the hubris of Lycaon and his sons, who had sacrificed a boy to Zeus. Zeus decided to end the Bronze Age and unleashed a deluge. Prometheus assisted his son Deucalion along with his wife Pyrrha (daughter of Epimetheus), by building a chest.


Shraddhadeva Hinduism 3rd-century CE
Shraddhadeva was the king of the Dravida Kingdom. Matsya (the avatar of Vishnu) first appeared as a shaphari (carp) while Shraddhadeva washed his hands in the river. Shraddhadeva saved the little fish, who kept growing until it filled the ocean. Vishnu revealed himself and informed Shraddhadeva of an impending all-destructive deluge. Shraddhadeva built a huge boat to house his family, saptarishi, seeds, and animals. Shesha appeared as a rope which the king used to fasten the boat to the horn of Vishnu. After the flood, the king repopulated the earth.


Cessair Irish 11th-century CE
Cessair was the daughter of Noah’s son Bith and his wife Birren. Noah either told them to go to the western edge of the world, or denied them a place on his ark. They created an idol who told them to escape the flood by sailing to Ireland. The only survivors of the journey are Cessair, 49 women, and 3 men. The women were split evenly amongst the men, but Bith and another die, leaving Fintán. He fled to a cave, and is the only survivor after the flood. He shapeshifts into a one-eyed salmon, then an eagle, and then a hawk.


Bergelmer Norse 13th-century CE
The gods Odin, Vili, and Vé slew Ymir (the progenitor of the jötnar). The slain Ymir proceeded to bleed so much that the whole race of frost-giants was drowned, excepting Bergelmer who escaped with his family. They boarded his lúðr and saved themselves in it.
