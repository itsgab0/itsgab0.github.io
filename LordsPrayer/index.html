What is the Bible?

The book called the Bible in English refers to the current Christian holy book. Generally, it's formed of two parts, called 'The Old Testament' and 'The New Testament'. The Old Testament is drawn from the Hebrew Bible and as such was written originally in Hebrew (more accurately called Biblical Hebrew, to distinguish it from the modern revived form of Hebrew). This part of the Bible can trace its origins as far back as the 8th century BCE.

In contrast, the New Testament was written some time after Jesus Christ's death, in roughly the 1stâ€”2nd century CE. Unlike the Old Testament, the scripture written here was in Koine Greek, a later form of Ancient Greek.

Translation History

The Romans (contemporaries of Jesus Christ) originally had a polytheistic religion which can trace many roots to the Ancient Greek religion. However, by the early 4th century CE, the Roman Empire had converted to Christianity. As such, the Bible was translated into Latin for a wider Roman audience.

They had conquered much of Great Britain by the mid-1st century CE, and maintained control until the early 5th century CE. Eventually they brought Christianity to the Britons, in Latin. However once the Romans left Britain the island was invaded by many Anglo-Saxons who set up their own kingdoms.

While there still existed a native Briton religion, the Anglo-Saxons brought many Nordic cultures and heritages with them. Attempts were made to retroactively link the earlier Anglo-Saxon kings to Odin, however these ideas have never caught on. Still, Christianity persisted in many parts, and in the 6th century, Christianity was brought again to Britain, and St. Augistine became the first Archbishop of Canterbury.

However, a problem arose. The people of Britain no longer spoke Latin natively, they spoke what we know call 'Old English', and so again the Bible had to be translated. In fact, as the language evolved slowly into the rough period known as 'Middle English' and into our own 'Modern English', the Bible was re-translated again and again. This gives a really pleasant way to see the evolution of the English Language.


What does this page offer?

On this page, the author hopes to detail some choice examples of the Lord's Prayer, taken from Matthew 6:12. Many of the examples provided were translated for active use throughout history, but some have been reconstructed anachronistically from Christianity's timeline, in places where Christianity either didn't yet exist, or in languages that we have reconstructed from later languages via the comparative method.

Reconstructed languages are prefaced with a * to indicate the fact that we have no extant writings of the language. This means that there is no "correct" way of translating the prayer into that language, and there is a high degree of disagreements even amongst the highly educated in this discipline. (Galilean Aramaic was the dialect of Jewish Palestinian Aramaic that would've been spoken by Jesus and his contemporaries from Galilee, and as such the reconstruction from Aramaic Rocks is especially interesting)

Aside from the 6 Main Examples (Modern English, Middle English, Old English, Latin, Koine Greek, Biblical Hebrew), the rest should be taken as a fun linguistic exercise rather than making misguided historiographical nightmares. The author has hand-transcribed an IPA pronunciation for the English languages using their own knowledge of the relevant phonologies, but for all other non-Latin script users, a transliteration into English will be provided and nothing more.

Additionally, many of the examples given here, especially the older Semitic ones, were hand-transcribed and digitised by the author from reading actual books and attempting to replicate the symbols used with very little personal understanding of the systems.

This page may also require additional non-standard fonts in order to render certain languages. The author has attempted to import them but some browsers may not do it automatically.

Brittonic Languages

Welsh is a Celtic language that has been spoken in Wales for centuries, despite preventative measures such as the 'Welsh Not' - a wooden board placed around the neck of a school student found to have spoken Welsh during Victorian times. Away from the British Isles is the closely-related Breton language, spoken in Brittany in France. Though number of speakers has dwindled it is still going, having even put forward a Breton entry to Eurovision. Cornish as a language died when the last native speaker John Davey died in 1891. However, it has since been revived by local authorities and is seeing a healthy uptake in speakers.
