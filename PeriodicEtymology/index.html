Periodic Table Etymology

What is the Periodic Table?

The Periodic Table is an ongoing project to categorise the chemical elements. Currently they're ordered based on their atomic number, in vertical 'groups' of identical valence electron numbers, and in horizontal 'periods' of identical valence shell inhabitation.

The person credited with creating the first Periodic Table was a Russian scientist called Dmitri Mendeleev (Дмитрий Менделеев).

Their novel arrangement of the known elements even left room for as-then undiscovered elements, such as the element below silicon which they named 'ekasilicon' - now known as germanium.

Over time, more and more elements have been discovered, codified and brought to public light, and so too has the periodic table expanded. However, the largest naturally-occuring element on planet Earth is uranium, element-92. This has meant that all greater elements have generally been created through one of two means:

  • Nuclear Fusion: Two atomic nuclei are fused at extreme man-made conditions to form one or more other nuclei.
  • Particle Collison: Two atomic nuclei are accelerated to extremely-high speeds and forced to collide, temporarily fusing into one or more other nuclei.

With each new discovery comes the question of what to call the new element. Naming rights have almost always gone to the creator/discoverer of the element, however some have opted to name an element after a famous person, usually another scientist and often one keenly involved in particle physics/chemistry.
